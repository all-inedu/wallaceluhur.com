Should blockchain technology be more widely used in society?

References

"Blockchain technology has the potential to boost global gross domestic product (GDP) by US$1.76 trillion over the next decade." (PWC, 2020). Nevertheless, what is blockchain technology? Blockchain is a decentralized, unchangeable database that makes tracking assets and recording transactions in a corporate network easier. Within a blockchain network, recording and trading nearly any valuable item becomes possible, which enhances efficiency and reduces risks for all involved parties (IBM, n.d.). Blockchain technology includes many features. One of its key features is decentralization. In the blockchain, decentralization refers to transferring control and decision-making from a centralized entity (individual, organization, or group thereof) to a distributed network (AWS, n.d.). Applying blockchain technology in any industry could improve traceability. When a transaction involving the swapping of goods is documented on a blockchain ledger, a traceable record is accessible to reveal the origins of the items involved. This can not only assist exchange-related organizations in increasing security and avoiding fraud, but it can also help confirm the legitimacy of the exchanged assets. It may be used to follow the supply chain from producer to distributor in sectors like pharmaceuticals, or it can be used to establish ownership in sectors like the arts. Secondly, blockchain eliminates the need for intermediaries in many procedures for industries like payments and real estate because of its decentralized nature. Blockchain enables quicker transactions by enabling P2P cross-border transfers with a digital currency compared to traditional financial services. A uniform system of ownership records and smart contracts that automate tenant-landlord agreements improve the efficiency of property management procedures (Koskal, 2019). Blockchain technology, as a decentralized and unchangeable database, holds the potential to revolutionize various industries by enhancing traceability and improving efficiency. Blockchain should be used more widely in society because by incorporating blockchain technology into various industries and aspects of society, we can benefit from increased security, transparency, efficiency, and traceability, ultimately driving innovation and economic growth. However, blockchain may face scalability, energy consumption, complexity, and regulatory uncertainties that must be addressed for widespread adoption.

Firstly, the economy will gain from the increasing use of blockchain technology. Between 2022 and 2030, the blockchain technology market is anticipated to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 85.9% (2021), expanding from a market valuation of USD 5.92 billion in 2021 and potentially contributing to a global GDP increase of up to US$1.76 trillion by 2030. Legalizing cryptocurrencies in nations like El Salvador and Ukraine is anticipated to open up new opportunities for market growth (Grandviewresearch.com, n.d.).

In the opinion of its proponents, blockchain technology, notably in the financial industry, can drastically increase the productivity and profitability of most firms, if not all of them—or perhaps transform the way we do business entirely.

The use of blockchain technology allows users worldwide to use financial services that they previously could not. People in emerging nations such as India, Nigeria, Kenya, and the Philippines are examples of emerging nations where blockchain technology could provide access to financial services for unbanked or underbanked populations, where it is more challenging to use regular banks, may utilize blockchain technology to get these services (PWC, 2020). China ($440 billion) and the USA ($407 billion) are the two countries with the most significant potential net benefits from blockchain.

Many developing countries lack the robust banking, internet, or electronic systems required to take advantage of the global digital transformation, but adopting blockchain technology and cryptocurrencies could be crucial to assisting these countries "leapfrog" — a process that involves utilizing new technologies to skip over traditional stages of infrastructure development and close gaps — and help these countries benefit from the global digital transformation (Getsmarter.com, 2022). To provide micro-loans to vendors in Kenya, IBM collaborated with Twiga Foods, an African business-to-business logistics network for kiosks and food stalls. These loans were designed to assist merchants in acquiring and controlling additional inventory. IBM's contribution was building a blockchain-enabled loan infrastructure that could assess the creditworthiness of food providers. Blockchain technology has been utilized to track toxin levels in Nigeria, where efforts are being made to clean up the river belt. These statistics are necessary for the reporting requirements of the international organizations supporting these initiatives (Reiff, 2021). By enabling nations to overcome technological and infrastructural barriers, promoting financial inclusion, and simplifying their integration into the global digital economy, the use of blockchain technology in developing nations can significantly increase economic growth.

However, blockchain technology and cryptocurrencies may occasionally still be erratic. A nation that only uses cryptocurrencies may experience volatility. Although stable currencies are employed, the stable currency of the "terra" crypto ecosystem failed. Blockchain technology is already having an evident impact on the world economy and financial policies. However, some of the consequences are harder to see. Worker replacement by efficient blockchain-based apps may increase unemployment rates in various industries. Of course, as cryptocurrency becomes more popular, the market's volatility is increasingly influencing global financial markets (Kriptomat.io, n.d.).

The U.S. Treasury Department cautioned that unregulated digital currencies might threaten the U.S. financial system. This warning was included in the inaugural comprehensive public report on digital assets published by the Treasury's Financial Stability Oversight Council. The council pinpointed crypto assets like stablecoins and the practice of lending and borrowing on the sector's trading platforms as a "significant emerging vulnerability." "important emerging vulnerability." According to Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen, in 2021, the report states that crypto-asset activities may present risks to the stability of the U.S. financial system. It highlights the significance of relevant regulation, including enforcing current laws. Yellen emphasized the necessity for government stakeholders to collaborate to advance the recommendations laid out in the report, referring to the situation as an "important emerging vulnerability" (U.S. Department of the Treasury, 2021). At the beginning of this year, the Treasury Department imposed a range of sanctions on Russian oligarchs, specific Russian banks, and other organizations, citing their use of crypto assets to circumvent sanctions. In September, the department froze all assets held or controlled by U.S. individuals, about 22 individuals, and two organizations that facilitated the digital funding of Russia's invasion of Ukraine (Cox, 2022). This measure demonstrates the government's commitment to combating illicit financial activities and ensuring that digital currency networks are not exploited maliciously.

Although blockchain can benefit economies, it can also significantly impact the environment. The New York Times (2022) claims that the yearly electricity consumption of the Bitcoin network is close to 91 terawatt-hours (91 TWh), which is more than that of nations like Finland. According to some reports, this amount is 150 TWh annually, equivalent to more energy than Argentina, a country with 45 million inhabitants (Clarke, 2022). Annualized power consumption from worldwide crypto-assets increased quickly between 2018 and 2022, according to projections. Published projections of the total yearly power consumption for crypto-assets as of August 2022 range from 120 to 240 billion kilowatt-hours, exceeding the total annual electricity consumption of several individual nations, such as Argentina or Australia. This is comparable to the yearly power use of all traditional data centers worldwide, representing 0.4% to 0.9% of the world's total annual electricity consumption (Whitehouse.gov, 2022). Because we use fossil-fired electricity, the highest market capitalization cryptocurrency assets generate 140-30 million metric tons of carbon dioxide yearly (Mt CO2/y), roughly 0.3% of the world's annual greenhouse gas emissions. According to estimates, the U.S. cryptocurrency market generates 25 to 50 Mt CO2/y, or 0.4% to 0.8% of the country's greenhouse gas emissions (Whitehouse.gov, 2022).

Proof of Work (PoW) and Proof of Stake (PoS) is consensus algorithms used in blockchain networks to validate and secure transactions. PoW relies on miners solving complex mathematical puzzles to validate transactions and create new blocks. This process requires significant computational power, which also consumes substantial energy (Nakamoto, 2008). PoS, on the other hand, selects validators based on their stake (amount of cryptocurrency held) and other factors, such as the duration of holding. PoS network evaluators propose new blocks and validate transactions without energy-intensive computations, making it a more eco-friendly alternative (Buterin, 2021).

The energy consumption of crypto assets is primarily driven by consensus mechanisms, such as distributed ledger technology, which is required for mining and validating transactions. The blockchains of Bitcoin and Ethereum both employ Proof of Work (PoW), the most popular consensus algorithm. More than 60% of the market value of all crypto assets is made up of Bitcoin and Ether combined. This feature prevents hostile actors from assaulting the network by making the PoW method more computationally intensive as more entities seek to validate transactions for coin rewards. Ethereum is projected to account for 20% to 39% of all global crypto-asset electricity usage by August 2022, while Bitcoin is expected to account for 60% to 77%. In addition to using grid electricity that has been purchased, crypto-asset mining operations may also have an impact on nearby noise and water quality, electronic waste, air and other pollution from any direct use of fossil-fired electricity, and additional air, water, and waste impacts from all grid electricity usage. These local impacts can exacerbate environmental justice issues for nearby communities, which are frequently already burdened by other pollutants, heat, traffic, or noise. When not directly utilizing renewable power, the development of energy-intensive crypto-asset technology might make it more difficult for the United States to meet its NDC under the Paris Agreement and prevent the worst effects of climate change. The federal government must take steps to promote and guarantee responsible development as crypto asset acceptance increases and new forms of digital assets may be introduced. This entails avoiding detrimental effects on neighborhood populations, dramatically lowering energy intensity, and using clean electricity as a source of energy (Whitehouse.gov, 2022).

However, according to (Clarke, 2022), there may not be a firm consensus on how mining cryptocurrencies on PoW networks impacts the environment. There has been a push to adopt blockchain to save energy and improve the environment. As a result, the utilization of renewable energy sources for Bitcoin mining has increased by over 60% this year. Another use of blockchain is to lower atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gasses like carbon dioxide. Blockchain technology, when applied to carbon credits, has the potential to benefit the environment significantly. R. A. Wilson, Chief Technical Officer of 1GCX, a global exchange for digital assets and carbon credits, argues that labeling Bitcoin as "bad" for the environment oversimplifies the issue and neglects important nuances and conversations. While it is true that proof-of-work networks, such as Bitcoin and others, consume more energy than proof-of-stake consensus algorithms, it is essential to consider other factors when evaluating the environmental impact of Bitcoin and blockchain technology as a whole (Clarke, 2022). Those who acknowledge these complexities may need to include the bigger picture and the potential for blockchain to contribute positively to environmental solutions.

In summary, blockchain technology has enormous potential to transform several industries and increase global GDP by US$1.76 trillion over the following ten years. Its decentralized and immutable nature improves efficiency and traceability, generating tremendous value for the global economy. Blockchain technology can increase financial inclusion in underdeveloped nations and help the environment through cutting-edge uses like carbon credits. By increasing productivity, lowering transaction costs, and promoting transparency across various industries, blockchain technology can increase GDP by promoting economic growth and innovation. However, challenges remain to be resolved, such as the volatility of cryptocurrencies and the negative impacts of energy-intensive consensus methods like Proof of Work on the environment. In order to explore different consensus mechanisms, such as Proof of Stake, which offers a more environmentally friendly solution, and to establish appropriate regulations to ensure the technology is not misused for bad intentions, more research is required as nations and businesses continue to adopt blockchain. It is critical to weigh the advantages and disadvantages of blockchain technology as we look back on its development and to support further research and development. By doing this, we can use the potential of blockchain to transform industries, improve people's lives, and help create a more equal and sustainable future.
