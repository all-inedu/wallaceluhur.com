Blockchain Beyond Bitcoin: Five Fascinating Applications

References

Blockchain technology, the magic behind Bitcoin, is like a digital diary that can safely record all kinds of transactions, and it's known for being very secure and open for everyone to see (Nakamoto, 2008). But, the fun fact is, blockchain is not just about cryptocurrencies, it can be used in many other ways too (Tapscott & Tapscott, 2016). This essay will show you five interesting ways blockchain can come in handy.

Making Supply Chains Better: Let's imagine a supply chain as a big team. This team's job is to take a product from the factory all the way to your doorstep. Since this team is large and the journey is long, things can get confusing and messy. Here's where blockchain comes to the rescue. By keeping a safe, unchangeable record of every single step, blockchain makes the whole process more organized and honest (Kshetri, 2018). A real-world example is the company De Beers. They use blockchain to track diamonds from the time they are mined to when they are sold, ensuring they aren't funding conflicts (De Beers Group, 2018).

Improving Voting Systems: Elections are the backbone of a democracy, and they need to be fair and secure. Blockchain could revolutionize voting by creating a safe, tamper-proof system for recording votes. This could make cheating almost impossible and encourage more people to vote, even from the comfort of their homes (Osgood, 2016). A project called Follow My Vote is already exploring this interesting use of blockchain.

Managing Healthcare Records: Imagine if all your health records were stored in a secure place and could be shared quickly among your doctors. This could lead to better, more coordinated care. Blockchain could make this possible by providing a secure platform to store and share health records (Krawiec et al., 2016). A company named MedRec is leading the charge in this area, using blockchain to create shared electronic health records.

Protecting Artists' Work: Artists and creators often find it hard to protect their work from being copied without their permission. But, blockchain could help. It can provide a clear, unchangeable record of who created a piece of work and who owns it (Boucher et al., 2017). This could help stop unauthorized copying and make sure artists get paid fairly. A good example is Mycelia, a platform created by musician Imogen Heap that uses blockchain to ensure fair payments for artists.

Checking Identities Online: Proving who you are online can be tricky and it's an easy target for fraudsters. But, blockchain technology could provide a safe way to prove your identity online, letting you control your personal information and who you share it with (ShoCard, 2015).

So, in a nutshell, blockchain technology is a superhero that can do much more than just deal with digital money. From making supply chains more efficient, securing votes, managing health records, protecting artists' work, to verifying identities online - blockchain can do it all. As this technology continues to grow, we can expect it to become even more exciting and useful.
